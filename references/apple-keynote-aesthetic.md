# Apple-Keynote Aesthetic

Read this file when the deck must feel premium, restrained, and presentation-led rather than report-led. Treat it as a practical review rubric for white-first leadership decks, not as a license to imitate Apple literally.

## Source Basis

These rules are distilled from a mix of official Apple materials and visual inference from Apple event and product presentation pages:

- Apple Human Interface Guidelines emphasize hierarchy, harmony, and consistency as core design values.
- Apple Design Resources provide the official typography and symbol systems used across Apple platforms.
- Apple Keynote release notes highlight professionally designed themes, layout and typography cleanup, minimalist themes, Auto Crop, SVG support, and chart cleanup.
- Apple Education presentation guidance emphasizes storytelling with typography, shapes, and clear visual structure.
- Apple event and product pages consistently use short headlines, oversized hero visuals, sparse supporting copy, and generous whitespace.

## Core Principles

### 1. One message per slide

- A slide should resolve to one takeaway in about two seconds.
- If the slide needs a paragraph to explain itself, the slide is under-designed.
- When in doubt, split the slide or cut content.

### 2. Hero-first composition

- Give the main proof, object, or result enough scale to dominate the page.
- Use one focal point, not multiple equal-weight clusters.
- On white slides, visual weight should come from scale, typography, and evidence rather than filled boxes.

### 3. Typography carries the brand

- Use a clean sans-serif with excellent numerals and consistent weights.
- Keep headlines short, declarative, and large.
- Let secondary text recede; captions should support, not compete.
- Use fewer text styles, not more.

### 4. Whitespace is structural

- Empty space is part of the layout system, not leftover area.
- Give breathing room around the hero object, title block, and chart labels.
- If a slide feels crowded, cut content before shrinking everything.

### 5. Color is restrained and purposeful

- Default to white or neutral backgrounds for most pages.
- Pull accent color from the hero image, chart highlight, or product identity rather than adding random palette blocks.
- Use dark backgrounds only when the image or section divider truly benefits from it.

### 6. Charts should look inevitable

- Remove non-essential gridlines, borders, legends, and labels.
- Highlight the one comparison that matters.
- Prefer direct labeling over distant legends when possible.
- The viewer should understand the contrast without a spoken walkthrough.

### 7. Motion should be quiet

- Prefer subtle fades, continuity, and gentle scale shifts.
- Avoid novelty transitions, unnecessary object choreography, and motion on every slide.
- If the deck would still feel premium with no motion, the design is probably strong enough.

### 8. Precision beats decoration

- Align edges exactly.
- Keep equal margins actually equal.
- Crop intentionally.
- Avoid shapes that feel almost centered, almost aligned, or almost balanced.

## What This Means For Leadership Decks

- Use conclusion-style titles, not topic labels.
- Make the evidence larger than the explanation.
- Show fewer metrics, but make the selected metric visually unavoidable.
- If a technical page exists, give it one business reason, one visual proof, and one short interpretation line.
- Use section divider slides sparingly; they should reset rhythm, not pad the deck.

## Apple-Like Review Checklist

Run this checklist before calling a deck visually done:

1. Can each slide be understood in about two seconds?
2. Does each slide have a single focal point?
3. Could at least 20 percent of the text be removed without losing meaning?
4. Does the slide still feel premium without colored cards or decorative shapes?
5. Is the hero object, number, or chart large enough?
6. Are margins, baselines, and object edges precise rather than approximate?
7. Is the chart obvious from a distance without verbal explanation?
8. If the slide is dark, is that choice clearly justified?
9. Does the deck rhythm alternate density and breathing room instead of keeping every page equally busy?
10. Would the page still feel strong if transitions were disabled?

## Anti-Patterns

- Multiple equal-weight cards fighting for attention
- Large paragraphs used to compensate for weak visuals
- Heavy gradients or glossy shapes on white slides
- Tiny charts surrounded by oversized explanatory text
- Decorative icons that do not carry meaning
- Random accent colors unrelated to the content
- Slides that only look acceptable when animated
